## 1. Signal: Shopware's Agentic Commerce Alliance — The European Standard for AI-to-AI Commerce

### The Open Protocol Stack That Could Define B2B AI Commerce

Shopware's Agentic Commerce Alliance (ACA), founded in July 2025, has published a detailed protocol stack for agentic commerce that deserves serious attention from anyone building AI agent products for European businesses.

**The five protocols:**
- **UCP** — Unambiguous Commerce Protocol (data exchange standard for product/catalog information between agents)
- **AXP** — Agent Exchange Protocol (for agent discovery and capability advertisement)
- **A2A** — Agent-to-Agent (direct communication between AI agents)
- **ACP** — Agent Commerce Protocol (transaction and order management)
- **AP2** — Agent Protocol Payments (payment execution and reconciliation)

**Shopware Nexus** is positioned as the "control center for agentic commerce" — a platform that understands a merchant's entire system landscape, detects dependencies, and automatically creates intelligent workflows. PayPal is already embedded in this ecosystem as a founding payment partner.

**The strategic insight**: This is not just about ecommerce. The ACA whitepaper "How AI Autonomy Will Redefine Ecommerce Competition" by Stefan Hamann describes a world where AI agents are the new buyers and sellers — not just tools for humans to use. B2B procurement is being reconceived as agent-to-agent negotiation.

**Use cases the ACA defines:**
- **Discovery**: Buyer agents interpret intent and match to suppliers/products without keywords
- **Constraint-aware decisions**: Agents automatically apply compatibility, compliance, availability, and contractual rules — eliminating invalid selections before checkout
- **Contextual pricing negotiation**: Agents evaluate customer value, volume, urgency, and history to determine pricing within predefined margins
- **Autonomous checkout execution**: Agents complete transactions on behalf of buyers, respecting thresholds and approval logic
- **Agentic customer service**: Agents resolve returns, replacements, refunds, and follow-ups automatically

**Assessment**: The ACA represents the most concrete European counterweight to US AI platform dominance in enterprise commerce. The open protocol approach (rather than proprietary lock-in) is directly aligned with Alize's positioning around sovereignty and interoperability.

**Implications for Alize:**
- French companies using Shopware or compatible systems (SAP Business One, Sage) are potential ACA-adjacent targets — Alize should be the managed service layer that helps them participate in agentic commerce
- The "agent as buyer" paradigm means French companies will need agents that can negotiate, procure, and sell on their behalf — this is a natural extension of Alize's managed service offering
- Alize's multi-model routing (Mistral for French compliance, Anthropic for capability) positions it well as the ACA-compliant managed service partner
- The A2A and AXP protocols are directly relevant to Alize's orchestration layer decisions (n8n vs. OpenClaw vs. custom)

## 2. Signal: Enterprise Multi-Agent Reality — 12 Agents Average, Half Still Working Alone

### The Orchestration Gap Is the Business Opportunity

Belitsoft's 2026 AI Agent Development Forecast, citing the Futurum Group's 1H 2026 Enterprise Software Decision Maker Survey, finds:

- **Enterprises run an average of 12 AI agents in production** — but approximately half of those agents operate without communicating with each other
- Agentic AI has become the **fastest-growing enterprise technology priority**, rising from 13.0% to 17.1% as a top-ranked priority
- The dominant deployment pattern is **multi-agent coordination** — not single-agent deployment

**LangChain's State of AI Agents Report (2025)** found that **53.5% of deployed AI professionals** cite productivity improvements as the primary motivation for deploying agents. But the average enterprise has 12 agents and half don't talk to each other — which means the productivity gains are fragmented.

**Key framing from Gartner**: By end of 2026, 40% of enterprise applications will include task-specific AI agents, up from less than 5% in 2025. This is an eightfold increase in a single year. But Gartner also predicts 40% of these projects will be canceled by 2027.

**The "autonomy gap"** — every time an AI agent fails and requires human intervention — is the gating factor between beachhead use cases and full labor replacement. The 12-agents-but-half-siloed data confirms that the autonomy gap is real and widespread.

**Assessment**: The orchestration problem is the business problem. Every company is buying agents; nobody has made them work together yet. This is the equivalent of the 1990s LAN era — everyone had PCs, nobody had reliable file sharing.

**Implications for Alize:**
- Alize's managed service positioning directly addresses the orchestration gap — "we deploy agents AND make them work together"
- The 40% project cancellation rate is a sales opportunity: companies failing alone need a partner who has done this before
- "12 agents, 6 siloed" is a concrete hook for Alize's first conversations: "How many agents do you have running, and do they talk to each other?"
- Multi-agent coordination is the premium tier of Alize's offering — after the first Quick Win establishes trust, orchestration is the upsell

## 4. Signal: Anthropic's "Building Effective Agents" Paper — The Definitive Taxonomy of Agent Types

### Workflow vs. Agent: The Distinction That Changes Everything

Anthropic published "Building Effective Agents" which has become a reference document in the industry. The key insight: **most enterprise "AI agents" are actually Workflows, not Agents**.

**Anthropic's taxonomy:**
- **Workflow**: A system where an LLM follows predefined code paths (if/else, loops, sequential steps). The LLM generates text/output, but the control flow is deterministic. Human has defined the path in advance.
- **Agent**: A system where an LLM dynamically determines its own path — perceiving the environment, reasoning about next steps, acting, and adapting in real-time without predetermined control flow.

**The practical implication**: Most enterprise AI deployments that claim to have "AI agents" are actually running Workflows. Real agents that self-direct are much harder to build, evaluate, and govern. Anthropic argues that for most production use cases, you want a Workflow, not an Agent.

**For Alize**: This validates Alize's Quick Win methodology. A "Quick Win" is by definition a narrowly scoped, well-defined workflow — not a fully autonomous agent. Starting with Workflows and graduating to Agents as the relationship matures is exactly the right approach.

**The 244-page Mythos System Card**: Chinese analysis of Anthropic's Mythos system card notes that it is 244 pages — exceptionally detailed for a model that was ultimately not released. The level of documentation signals Anthropic's shift toward ultra-transparent safety evaluation (learned from the Claude 3 Opus safety controversy). This matters for enterprise trust: Anthropic is trying to demonstrate that they take safety seriously enough to leave a model on the shelf.
