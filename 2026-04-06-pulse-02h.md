## Signal 1: The SMB Adoption Gap — 48% Enterprise vs 4% SMB Using Agentic AI

ADP's 2025 HR trends survey surfaced a striking adoption gap: **48% of large businesses** are now using agentic AI, compared to just **4% of small businesses**. The gap is not primarily about budget. It's about organizational capacity to redesign workflows, retrain managers, and absorb liability questions that come with systems acting without per-step human approval.

McKinsey's workforce research reinforces the scale: the number of workers in roles where AI fluency is explicitly required has grown **7x in two years** — from ~1 million in 2023 to ~7 million in 2025. That is not a gradual transition. It is a fundamental reconfiguration of what "qualified" means, happening faster than most HR functions can track.

Deloitte's 2026 Tech Trends report (titled "the agentic reality check") draws the line between genuine deployment and theater. Most enterprise agentic AI in production operates in **three narrow categories**: document processing/summarization, code generation/review, and customer-facing chatbots with escalation paths. The broader vision of agents autonomously managing cross-system workflows, negotiating with external parties, and making resource-allocation decisions is real in pilots but rare in production.

**The constraint is not primarily technical. It is governance.** Enterprises lack audit trails, rollback mechanisms, and liability frameworks to let autonomous systems act at scale in regulated environments.

**What this means for Alizé:**

This is the most direct validation of the managed service model. Large enterprises (48%) can afford to build internal AI governance capabilities. Small businesses (4%) cannot — but they face the same liability questions. Alizé's value proposition is precisely bridging that gap: taking the organizational complexity of AI adoption off the PME's plate.

The 7x growth in AI-fluency job requirements also points to a talent gap Alizé can help fill. French PME don't need to hire prompt engineers — they need an operational partner who handles the agent-interface work for them.

## Signal 2: Entry-Level Hiring Disrupted — 64% of Orgs Changed Approach Due to AI Agents

Gartner identified AI revolution and cost pressures as the two forces driving the top talent acquisition trends for 2026. The behavioral evidence is concrete: **64% of organizations have already altered their entry-level hiring approach due to AI agents**, up from 18% the prior quarter.

PwC's 2025 Global AI Jobs Barometer found that workers with AI skills command **wage premiums up to 56% higher** than peers doing equivalent work without those skills. That premium will compress as supply catches up, but in 2026 it's real — enterprises not paying it are losing their strongest candidates.

Korn Ferry's TA Trends 2026 research: **52% of talent leaders plan to add AI agents to their teams in 2026**. The framing is important — these organizations are not reducing headcount. They are increasing the output of existing teams by adding autonomous capacity alongside human workers.

The restructuring in practice: humans do more judgment, verification, and exception-handling; agents handle repetitive throughput work.

**New roles at the human-agent interface:** People who write effective agent instructions, evaluate agent output quality, catch errors before they compound, and explain agent decisions to non-technical stakeholders. These skills don't yet have settled job titles or standard compensation benchmarks. Enterprises building these capabilities in-house are ahead of the market; enterprises waiting for the market to define the roles before hiring will be 18 months behind.

**What this means for Alizé:**

Alizé is essentially an "agent-interface-as-a-service" company. Every engagement involves writing agent instructions, designing escalation logic, evaluating outputs, and translating agent decisions for non-technical stakeholders. These are exactly the roles enterprises are struggling to build internally. Alizé's operational team is the client's agent-interface capability, on demand.

Also: as AI disrupts entry-level hiring, French PME may have a harder time competing for junior talent with AI skills. Alizé can position the service as "your team's AI fluency partner" — bridging the capability gap without requiring the client to hire scarce AI-fluent workers.

**What this means for Alizé:**

The bug likely reflects Anthropic making the Claude Code agentic behavior more compute-intensive (more agentic loops = more API calls). If this is a permanent change rather than a bug, it means:
- Agentic AI is more expensive per task than raw API pricing suggests
- The value of prompt optimization, output evaluation, and avoiding wasted agent loops is higher than assumed
- Alizé's managed service model (with trained operators who write efficient agent instructions) has more value if clients would otherwise burn through quotas with suboptimal agent behavior

Louis should monitor this for the Alizé cost model. If session exhaustion is the new normal for agentic usage, Alizé's service pricing needs to account for efficient agent operations rather than raw token counts.
