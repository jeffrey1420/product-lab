## Signal 1: College Instructors Using Typewriters to Fight AI-Written Work

**Source:** Hacker News, item #47818485 (281 points, 15 hours ago)

A college instructor has switched to typewriters as the primary assessment tool to combat AI-generated student work. The story is trending on HN and sparking a broad discussion about AI detection failure in education.

**HN discussion highlights:**
- "When I did my CS degree, programming exams were handwritten — in pencil and paper. The beneficial prep was using punch cards with 24-hour compile turnaround. That really makes you think."
- "We already had AI-proof education" — argument that in-person, handwritten, proctored exams are the original design
- Best comment: instructor deliberately sabotaged physical networks with barely-unscrewed DIN connectors to test troubleshooting skills — hardware-based assessment that no AI can fake

**Why this matters for Alizé:**

This is not just an education story — it's a **proxy war for AI governance**. The instructor's underlying problem: they cannot distinguish AI-assisted work from human work, and have no reliable audit trail. This is structurally identical to the enterprise problem Alizé is solving: when AI agents act on behalf of employees, how do you know who did what, when, and whether the output reflects genuine human oversight?

The typewriter gambit is a red flag for the broader economy:
- **Knowledge work is experiencing the same audit crisis as academic work.** If universities — institutions whose core function is evaluating human knowledge — can't solve this internally, the pressure on enterprises to adopt AI governance tools will intensify.
- **Human-in-the-loop is becoming a legal and reputational requirement**, not just a best practice. French universities (and the broader EU academic ecosystem) will face this acutely under EU AI Act provisions for AI in education.
- **Provenance infrastructure becomes a market.** The ability to cryptographically verify "this document was written by a human at this time with this input" is becoming a real business requirement, not just a theoretical one.

**Alizé angle:** Alizé's audit trail and governance layer is exactly the provenance infrastructure the broader knowledge economy is scrambling to build. The typewriter instructor is doing manually what Alizé automates: establishing a chain of custody for human-generated work.

## Signal 2: AI System Passes Peer Review — Scientific Publishing Enters Crisis Mode

**Source:** Forbes (April 13, 2026), covered in humai.blog digest

An AI system successfully passed peer review at a scientific journal — raising fundamental questions about the future of scientific publishing and research validation. UC Berkeley's Carl Boettiger criticized the AI Scientist project for focusing on writing papers rather than conducting meaningful research.

**Key data points:**
- The AI Scientist project (which automatically generates and submits papers) is now generating publishable-quality output
- Academic journals are being asked to distinguish AI-authored from human-authored submissions
- Core critique: the AI is gaming the existing publishing infrastructure rather than advancing science

**Why this matters for Alizé:**

The peer review crisis is a governance problem disguised as an academic problem:
1. **If AI can pass peer review, the publish-or-perish economy is now partially automated** — creating distortions in knowledge markets
2. **Scientific credibility is at stake.** Institutions relying on published research (regulators, standards bodies, enterprise R&D) face a new validation problem
3. **Alizé's "human oversight" positioning gains credibility in adjacent markets** — not just enterprise operations, but research integrity, regulatory compliance, and knowledge certification

This is a longer-cycle signal but reinforces the broader trend: governance, audit, and provenance are becoming load-bearing infrastructure for institutions that rely on trustworthy knowledge.

## Signal 3: Social Media AI Securities Fraud Risk — Platforms as AI Makers

**Source:** Bloomberg Law (April 14, 2026), covered in humai.blog digest

Northern District of California ruled that when platforms' AI exercises "ultimate authority" over assembled ad content, they may be considered makers of fraudulent statements under Rule 10b-5. This creates primary liability for Meta, Alphabet, Snap, TikTok, and X Corp for AI-generated investment solicitations.

**Why this matters for Alizé:**

1. **"AI as maker" doctrine has arrived.** If a platform's AI can be legally liable for content it assembles, enterprises deploying AI agents need to ask: who is the "maker" when our agent generates a recommendation, a response, or a decision?
2. **This is directly applicable to Alizé's French SME clients.** French financial regulators (AMF) and the EU's Markets in Crypto-Assets (MiCA) regulation will likely follow the same logic. Any French PME using AI to generate investment-adjacent content (product recommendations, pricing decisions, market communications) could face analogous liability.
3. **Alizé's governance layer becomes a legal risk mitigation tool**, not just an operational efficiency one. Audit trails that demonstrate human oversight could be the difference between primary liability and protected advisory activity.
