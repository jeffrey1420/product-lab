## Signal 1: Databricks State of AI Agents — Supervisor Agents at 37%, 327% Multi-Agent Growth

**Source:** Databricks Blog (April 2026), State of AI Agents report (20,000+ global customers)

Databricks published its 2026 State of AI Agents report with findings from over 20,000 enterprise customers. The data is the most comprehensive enterprise AI agent deployment picture available right now.

**Key findings:**

- **327% growth in multi-agent workflows** — enterprises are rapidly moving beyond single-agent deployments to orchestrated multi-agent systems
- **Supervisor Agent = 37% of all agent usage on Databricks Agent Bricks** — the dominant pattern is a supervisor that orchestrates specialized sub-agents across domains. This is the "air traffic control" model
- **AI agents now create 80% of all databases on Neon (serverless Postgres)** and **97% of database branches** — AI-generated database management at this scale is a leading indicator of how deeply agents are infiltrating infrastructure ops
- **40% of top 15 AI use cases focus on customer experience and engagement** — the frontline of enterprise AI deployment is customer-facing
- **12x more AI projects reach production with governance tools** — this is the statistic Alizé should print on its homepage
- **6x more AI systems reach production with evaluation tools** — quality assurance tooling is a prerequisite for production, not an afterthought
- **40% of respondents say their organization's AI governance is insufficient** (Economist Impact, 2024) — and this was before agent proliferation accelerated

**Industry-specific use cases:**
- Healthcare/life sciences: medical literature analysis
- Automotive/energy/utilities: predictive maintenance
- Financial services: regulatory reporting, market intelligence

**Alizé angle:**

The 12x production multiplier with governance tools is the single most compelling Alizé value proposition number. It means: without governance, your AI projects stall. With governance, they ship.

The Supervisor Agent dominance (37%) validates the "orchestration layer" framing for Alizé. Alizé's managed service IS a supervisor agent layer — it supervises the agents that supervisors deploy. This is the governance layer two levels up.

The database creation stat (80% AI-generated) is a glimpse at a future where infrastructure itself is agent-maintained. When AI agents are creating and branching databases autonomously, the governance challenge becomes "who audits the agents that manage the infrastructure?"

**Takeaway:** Enterprise AI is in the multi-agent orchestration phase. The bottleneck is no longer deployment — it's governance. 12x production multiplier with governance = Alizé's core pitch.

## Signal 2: Anthropic Identity Verification — Platform-Level Trust Layer for Claude

**Source:** Zhihu / Chinese tech press (5 days ago, so ~April 15, 2026)

Anthropic is rolling out identity verification on the Claude platform. Users accessing certain features or triggering routine platform integrity checks will now be required to pass through third-party identity verification partners.

This is a significant platform-level decision that signals a shift from "anyone with an API key" to "verified identity required for sensitive operations." It has direct implications for:

- **Agent identity**: if agents operate under verified human identities, accountability chains become clearer
- **Enterprise procurement**: companies buying Claude Enterprise seats will need identity-compliant provisioning
- **Compliance**: French companies under NIS2 or GDPR frameworks may benefit from platform-level identity requirements

**Alizé angle:**

Anthropic's identity verification move is the infrastructure layer doing what compliance frameworks will eventually mandate at the regulatory level. Alizé should track this: if Anthropic is verifying human identities, can agents have their own verifiable identities? And if agents have identities, who governs those identities?

For Alizé's French mid-market prospects (PME/ETI), platform-level identity verification from Anthropic reduces one compliance burden — the model provider is handling identity attestation. Alizé's governance layer then operates on top, managing agent permissions, tool access, and operational oversight.

**Takeaway:** Anthropic is building trust infrastructure at the platform level. Alizé operates at the operational governance layer above that — complementary, not competitive.

## Signal 3: Claude Mythos — The Model That Required a 24-Hour Internal Review Before Release

**Source:** Zhihu / Chinese AI community (April 8, 2026)

Anthropic's rumored next model, Claude Mythos (also referred to as Opus 4.7 or a separate model), reportedly required a dedicated 24-hour internal review window before being connected to internal systems. The concern: Anthropic's own infrastructure team was not confident the model wouldn't cause harm to Anthropic's own systems.

This is a notable data point: **the model's own creators didn't fully trust it before deployment**. This mirrors what we saw with Claude Code's autonomy doubling (25 min → 45 min sessions) — capability is outpacing safety guarantees in production.

**For context:**
- Claude Opus 4.6 reportedly shows significant capability degradation in iterative project design tasks — "能力退化" (capability regression) was noted in Chinese reviews
- Codex (coding agent) is described as powerful for bug finding but weak at code writing, "惨不忍睹" (hard to bear witness to)
- The Mythos review window suggests Anthropic is being more cautious with frontier model releases than with agent product releases

**Alizé angle:**

If even Anthropic's own infrastructure team needed a 24-hour safety review before Mythos deployment, what should PME companies be doing before deploying agents on critical business processes? This is the governance gap, stated plainly. Alizé's "diagnostic before deployment" positioning becomes even more credible when the model's own creator needed a safety review.

**Takeaway:** Frontier model capability is outpacing safety guarantees even at Anthropic. This gap = Alizé's market.
