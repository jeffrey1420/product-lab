## Signal 1: Claude Mythos Is Rewriting Cybersecurity — Machine-Speed Defense Becomes Table Stakes

**Sources:** CSO Online (April 13), Colitco (April 11), TechCrunch HumanX coverage (April 12)

Claude Mythos Preview continues to reverberate through the enterprise security world. IBM executives are calling it a generational shift — the UK's AI Security Institute evaluation found Mythos outperformed all other AI systems in complex attack scenarios and capture-the-flag challenges. The critical finding: the window between vulnerability discovery and weaponization has collapsed from weeks to hours.

OpenAI responded with GPT-5.4-Cyber (April 14) — a restricted-access cybersecurity model, guardrails lowered for security research. The two AI labs are now in a direct race to own the enterprise/government cybersecurity agent category.

CSO Online's framing is the important one for Alizé: current patch cycles and incident response processes were not designed for this environment. Organizations need "Mythos-ready" security programs that can respond at machine speed, not human timescales.

**Alizé angle:**

This is validation of Alizé's core premise at the infrastructure level. If vulnerability discovery is now machine-speed, then governance and oversight must also operate at machine speed. The product's human-in-the-loop layer needs to be positioned not as "slowing things down" but as "enabling safe machine-speed operation." The framing shift: from "AI agents need oversight" to "you can't have AI agents without governance infrastructure."

More concretely: Alizé's diagnostic offering should include a "Mythos-readiness assessment" — does the prospect's current agent fleet have the governance infrastructure to respond to a vulnerability disclosure at machine speed?

**Takeaway:** The cybersecurity implications of faster AI agents are Alizé's single most important category validation this month. Claude Mythos + GPT-5.4-Cyber = the market agreeing that the agents-have-keys problem is now existential.

## Signal 3: Self-Evolving Agents Are Here — MiniMax M2.7 Hits 56% on SWE-Pro

**Source:** MarkTechPost (April 12)

MiniMax open-sourced M2.7, a self-evolving agent model that iteratively improves its own performance over 24-hour windows. Architecture: short-term memory + self-feedback + self-optimization. Scores 56.22% on SWE-Pro (coding), 57.0% on Terminal Bench 2. Matches Gemini-3.1 (66.6% medal rate), trails only Claude Opus 4.6 (75.7%) and GPT-5.4 (71.2%).

**RED FLAG for Alizé/Louis (from MEMORY):** Louis's Claude Code setup is using MiniMax as the API provider. MiniMax was named in the US-China model distillation war (MiniMax, DeepSeek, Moonshot cited by US AI trio as targets). If distillation = IP theft concern, French/European companies using MiniMax API for agentic workflows may face regulatory scrutiny. This is a positioning risk for any Alizé prospect using MiniMax.

**Also note:** Self-evolving agents that improve autonomously over 24-hour windows are exactly the kind of agent that requires strong governance. Without oversight, a self-improving agent could drift from its intended task scope in ways humans can't easily audit.

**Takeaway:** Alizé should track self-evolving agent capabilities as a product requirement. The governance layer needs to handle agents that change their behavior over time, not just agents that execute fixed tasks.

## Signal 4: AI Agent Market Hits $10.91B — But 62% Are Still Experimenting, Scaling Remains Rare

**Source:** Ringly.io aggregate (Gartner, McKinsey, Salesforce, Deloitte, IBM)

Key numbers:
- Global AI agents market: $10.91B in 2026, projected $50.31B by 2030 (45.8% CAGR)
- 51% of enterprises have AI agents in production (up from much lower)
- **62% of organizations experimenting with AI agents, but fewer than 10% are scaling them**
- Gartner: 40% of enterprise apps will embed task-specific AI agents by end of 2026
- Gartner: **Over 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027** due to escalating costs, unclear value, weak risk controls
- Average ROI: 171% on agentic deployments; US enterprises hit 192%

The gap between "experimenting" (62%) and "scaling" (<10%) is the market's biggest problem. Gartner's 40%+ cancellation prediction is a direct validation of Alizé's governance positioning — weak risk controls are killing projects.

**Alizé angle:**

The diagnostic → pilot → managed service model maps directly to this problem. Companies stuck in experimentation need help getting to production. Companies seeing cancellations need help understanding why. The diagnostic phase is the entry point; the managed service is the recurring revenue.

The 40% cancellation number is a sales conversation opener: "Gartner says 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled this year. What's your cancellation risk?"

**Takeaway:** The market is big and growing. The problem is not adoption — it's successful scaling. Alizé's positioning as "the reason your AI agents don't get cancelled" is directly supported by these numbers.

## Signal 5: Neuro-Symbolic AI Gains Credibility — Hybrid Architectures Are the Next Wave

**Source:** Forbes (April 12)

A leak of Anthropic's Claude Code components legitimized neuro-symbolic AI — combining neural networks (data-driven, pattern recognition) with symbolic/expert systems (rules-based, logic). Forbes frames this as conventional LLM approaches potentially hitting their limits, making hybrid architectures increasingly attractive.

**Alizé angle:**

Neuro-symbolic architectures are actually easier to govern than pure neural agents. Rules-based components can be audited, logged, and reasoned about. This is good news for Alizé's feasibility. A future where enterprise agents are part neural, part symbolic is a future where governance infrastructure can be both flexible (neural) and auditable (symbolic).

**Takeaway:** Less immediate impact on Alizé, but longer-term product direction should consider how to support hybrid architectures in the governance layer.

## Signal 6: Physical AI Breakthrough — Robots That Handle Cash

**Source:** CNET (April 11)

Generalist AI's Gen-1 physical AI model can handle cash, sort socks, stack objects — dextrous manipulation tasks that require fine motor skills humans take for granted. Trained using human demonstration data (humans wearing specialized data-collection tech), not teleoperation.

This is a preview of the next category of AI agent: physical world task automation. French manufacturing, logistics, and retail could be early adopters.

**Alizé angle:**

Physical AI agents introduce a new governance dimension: what happens when an AI agent physically damages something? Current digital governance frameworks (access control, audit logs, approval workflows) don't cover physical world consequences. This is a future Alizé opportunity — governance for physical AI agents, not just digital ones.

**Source:** Geeky Gadgets (April 11)
