## Signal 1: Microsoft Copilot — "For Entertainment Purposes Only"

The most significant enterprise AI liability story in recent memory broke April 5, 2026. TechCrunch reported that Microsoft's Copilot terms of use contain language stating: **"Copilot is for entertainment purposes only. It can make mistakes, and it may not work as intended. Don't rely on Copilot for important advice. Use Copilot at your own risk."** This language was last updated October 24, 2025.

Microsoft is simultaneously charging enterprises for Copilot while legally disclaiming it as unsuitable for serious work. A Microsoft spokesperson told PCMag the language is "legacy" and will be updated — but the current terms create a direct liability gap for any French PME using Copilot as their primary AI tool.

- The comparison to medical device disclaimers ("For Research Use Only. Not for diagnostic procedures") is apt — the companies selling the most are the ones covering their backs legally.

Alizé's positioning — operational IA with governance, oversight, and accountability — directly addresses the liability gap that Microsoft has just publicly admitted exists.

## Signal 2: Japan Proves Physical AI Works — But Only for Jobs Nobody Wants

TechCrunch's April 5 report on Japan's physical AI deployment is the most concrete evidence that AI-powered robots are ready for real-world deployment — but only in contexts where humans don't care about AI error. Japan's Ministry of Economy, Trade and Industry aims to capture 30% of the global physical AI market by 2040, driven by a 14th consecutive year of population decline and a working-age population that will shrink by 15 million in 20 years.

Key quote from the article: *"The driver has shifted from simple efficiency to industrial survival."* — Sho Yamanaka, Principal, Salesforce Ventures.

The pattern: Japan is deploying physical AI in factories, warehouses, and logistics — environments where errors are contained and recoverable. This mirrors what Deloitte said about enterprise agentic AI: narrow, bounded, repetitive tasks are ready; open-ended judgment tasks are not.

Mujin (Japanese robotics startup) is notable — their approach is software-first: a robotics control platform that makes existing hardware perform more autonomously. Not building new robots, making existing robots smarter.

**What this means for Alizé:**

Not directly applicable to Alizé's service model, but the analogy is precise. Alizé should follow the same playbook Mujin uses: deploy AI into bounded, repetitive PME workflows where errors are contained and recoverable. Don't sell the vision of an AI that handles open-ended judgment — sell the robot that reliably handles the 40 invoices your accounting team hates processing every month.

The Japan story also validates Alizé's geographic timing: France doesn't have Japan's demographic crisis yet, but the structural labor shortage in service and logistics sectors is real. French PME adopting AI now are positioning for the same survival calculus Japan is living.
