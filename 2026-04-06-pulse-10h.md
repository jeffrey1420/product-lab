## Signal 1: The "SaaSpocalypse" — Oliver Wyman Declares the SaaS Model Under Siege

The most strategically significant analysis this cycle comes from Oliver Wyman (April 2026): agentic AI is no longer being priced as a SaaS growth accelerator — it's being priced as a **SaaS workflow replacement**. Public software stocks have been repricing since early 2026, with the market repricing three core assumptions:

1. **"Software is hard to build"** → AI agents can now build software cheaply and quickly, commoditizing the development moat
2. **"Seat expansion is durable"** → AI agents do the work of people, reducing seat counts and their associated revenue
3. **"Features create moats"** → Agentic development commoditizes features; agents interface directly with software, bypassing the UI entirely

The critical quote: *"The fear is that while software economics migrate, many SaaS companies are priced for a world that no longer exists."*

Private equity is now scrutinizing AI exposure in deal processes, and lenders are asking harder questions about revenue durability. This is not a temporary correction — it's a structural repricing of software business models.

**What this means for Alizé:**

This is a double-edged sword. Alizé's managed service model sits *between* the SaaS layer being disrupted and the AI agent layer doing the disruption. 

On one side: SaaS vendors being disintermediated by AI agents could mean fewer seats sold, which hurts traditional software revenue. Alizé benefits from this disruption — if clients need AI orchestration and governance *instead of* more SaaS licenses, that's Alizé's opening.

On the other side: if AI agents become sophisticated enough to replace Alizé's operational service layer (i.e., "just buy the agent, you don't need a managed service"), Alizé faces the same SaaSpocalypse risk.

**The answer** is what Alizé already knows: the moat is not the AI tooling — it's the human-in-the-loop governance, the accountability structure, and the domain expertise to design bounded workflows. Oliver Wyman's analysis actually validates this: enterprises need someone to own the agent, verify its outputs, and be accountable when it fails. That's Alizé's layer. The "control center" layer Deloitte described — tracking usage, expenditures, access, performance, security, and compliance across multiple agents — is precisely Alizé's managed service positioning.

The SaaSpocalypse is Alizé's opportunity, not its threat. But the pitch needs to be explicit: Alizé isn't a software subscription, it's an **operational AI partnership with accountability baked in**. That framing survives the SaaSpocalypse; the "AI-powered SaaS tool" framing does not.

## Signal 3: Deloitte — AI Agent Pricing Is Shifting to Outcomes, Not Seats

Deloitte's 2026 SaaS + AI agents analysis surfaces a critical pricing dynamic: the industry is moving (slowly) from seat-based and subscription pricing toward **outcome-based pricing** — paying for real business results like tickets resolved, contracts reviewed, or revenue contributed to.

The challenge Deloitte identifies is significant:
- Defining "an agent," "a task," "a process," "an interaction," and "an outcome" contractually is hard
- Multi-agent systems from different vendors make attribution difficult
- Revenue for vendors and costs for customers could become "less predictable and highly variable"
- System instrumentation and metering may need to become "more advanced and data observability, billing, and financial compliance may have to become more real time and autonomous"

Deloitte also predicts that SaaS UX will shift to become more **conversational and diagnostic** — natural language replacing menus and clicks, and transparency/explainability becoming crucial for trust.

**What this means for Alizé:**

Alizé's managed service model is actually an early realization of this outcome-based pricing trend. Instead of charging per-seat or per-feature, Alizé charges for AI operations that produce business outcomes — with human oversight baked in. This is the correct model, but it's ahead of the market.

The challenge is measurement. If Alizé can't prove its agents produced measurable outcomes (X invoices processed, Y customer responses handled, Z% reduction in response time), it gets commoditized into a time-and-materials consulting engagement rather than a valuedriven managed service.

**Action item for Alizé**: Start building outcome metrics now. For each client engagement, track: tasks completed autonomously, tasks escalated to humans, error rate, time saved. This becomes the evidence base for outcome-based pricing conversations and defensibility against "just use Copilot" objections.

## Signal 4: Syntaqlite — The Real AI-Assisted Coding Playbook (3 Months vs 8 Years)

The HN front page this cycle featured a detailed retrospective from a developer who spent "eight years of wanting, three months of building with AI" to create Syntaqlite — a tool for querying databases via natural language. This is the most honest public account of what AI-assisted production coding actually looks like in 2026.

Key patterns the author discovered:

**Phase 1 (vibe coding)**: AI generates working code fast. The MVP was built in weeks. "It looked professional. The equations seemed right." This matches the pattern of every AI coding success story.

**Phase 2 (the reckoning)**: When reviewing the codebase seriously, it was "complete spaghetti." Functions scattered in random files. Files grown to several thousand lines. Fragile. The AI had solved the immediate problem but created a foundation that "was never going to cope with the larger vision."

**Phase 3 (human salvage)**: The author went through error by error with AI assistance. Added type checking. Fixed 90 type errors in ~2 hours. Tightened abstractions. The pattern: AI as "chainsaw" for initial cutting, humans for precision finishing.

HN comments surfaced the emerging best practice for AI-assisted coding:
- Use AI for ideation and architecture (have AI poke holes in reasoning)
- Use AI for first-pass implementation
- **Always review the code** — "AI is always very loose on requirements"
- AI fields are "nullable by default"; records have "little to no validation"
- AI reaches for "brittle async solutions" (LISTEN/NOTIFY, callbacks) instead of architecturally correct ones
- Type checking, linting, and formal pre-commit hooks are non-negotiable for production

**What this means for Alizé:**

Alizé is not a coding shop, but this pattern applies to AI agent design. The "vibe coding" equivalent for AI agents is "vibe prompting" — writing prompts that seem right but haven't been stress-tested against edge cases. Alizé's review-and-refine process for client agent workflows should follow the same discipline: initial prompt → adversarial testing → human review of outputs → formal error escalation logic.

The "Schwartz catching Claude faking results" thread (from the "comfortable drift" article also on HN front page this cycle) is the same pattern in reverse: an expert is needed to catch AI producing plausible-but-wrong outputs. For Alizé, the "Schwartz" is the human operator reviewing agent outputs — and that human oversight is not a cost center, it's the quality assurance layer that makes the whole system trustworthy.

## Signal 5: Caveman Tokens — When Less Output Is More Signal

"Caveman" (766 points on HN) is a lightweight technique that strips LLM output to caveman-speak (short, direct, minimal preamble) — achieving ~75% token reduction with preserved task success in some domains. The author notes this isn't about saving hidden reasoning tokens — it's about reducing visible completion overhead.

HN commenters discovered an unexpected benefit: seeing ideas framed in caveman-speak sometimes provides genuine conceptual clarity. "It cuts through bullshit just a smidge." The brevity forces the AI to state only what matters.

**What this means for Alizé:**

For Alizé's agent outputs — especially client-facing summaries, reports, and decision-support documents — the Caveman principle is relevant. AI agents should produce outputs that are direct and action-oriented, not verbose and hedged. A client's invoice processing agent doesn't need a three-paragraph preamble before listing which invoices were approved. 

Alizé can build a prompt engineering discipline around output compression: structured, minimal outputs for operational tasks (invoice approved: amount X, vendor Y, date Z); expanded outputs only for ambiguous cases requiring human judgment. This is an underexplored angle of the operational efficiency story.
