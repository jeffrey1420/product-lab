## Signal 1: Vercel Breach via Third-Party AI Tool — Context.ai OAuth Compromise

**Sources:** BleepingComputer (April 19), Vercel Security Bulletin (April 19), HN (661 points)

Vercel disclosed a security incident on April 19 after the ShinyHunters threat actor claimed to be selling stolen data. The breach vector is the most relevant detail for Alizé: **the initial access came through a compromised third-party AI tool, Context.ai.**

Specifically:
- A Vercel employee's Google Workspace account was compromised via a breach at Context.ai (a third-party AI tool)
- Context.ai's Google Workspace OAuth application was broadly compromised, affecting "hundreds of users across many organizations"
- The attacker used the compromised OAuth app to access the employee's Google Workspace account, then escalated into Vercel environments
- Non-sensitive environment variables (not marked "sensitive" in Vercel's system) were enumerated and accessed
- Vercel stores environment variables marked "sensitive" encrypted at rest — those were not accessed
- Attacker claims to have access keys, source code, database data, NPM tokens, GitHub tokens
- Vercel is working with Mandiant, law enforcement, and has published IOCs

ZDNET framed it as a "cybersecurity Manhattan Project"-level threat. The lesson is direct: **third-party AI tools are now a primary supply chain attack vector.** Context.ai is not a rogue vendor — it's a legitimate AI productivity tool used by technical teams.

**Alizé angle:**

This is the most concrete supply-chain-via-AI-tool breach documented in 2026. Alizé's diagnostic framework should include a "third-party AI tool audit" as a standard module. Prospects need to understand that the attack surface isn't just their own agent deployments — it's every AI tool their employees connect via OAuth.

The OAuth permission model is the weak point: when an employee authenticates to an AI tool via Google OAuth, that tool gets potentially broad access to the employee's Google account data, which can then be pivoted into connected systems (as happened with Vercel). Alizé's governance layer should address OAuth permission scoping for AI tools.

**Takeaway:** Third-party AI tool governance just became an immediate, concrete problem — not theoretical. Alizé should develop a specific assessment module for "AI tool supply chain risk."

## Signal 2: Project Glasswing — Anthropic's $100M AI-Defensive Security Coalition

**Sources:** Anthropic.com/glasswing (April 7), ZDNET (April 7), Tech-Insider.org (April 10)

Anthropic announced Project Glasswing on April 7 — a major initiative to use Claude Mythos Preview (an unreleased frontier model) for defensive cybersecurity. The coalition includes AWS, Apple, Broadcom, Cisco, CrowdStrike, Google, JPMorganChase, Linux Foundation, Microsoft, NVIDIA, and Palo Alto Networks. Anthropic is committing $100M in Mythos Preview usage credits plus $4M in direct donations to open-source security organizations.

Key facts from the announcement:
- Mythos Preview has found thousands of high-severity vulnerabilities across every major OS and web browser
- Some vulnerabilities survived decades of human review and millions of automated security tests
- The attack window has collapsed from months to minutes with AI
- Over 40 additional organizations building critical infrastructure get access to scan and secure systems
- The initiative extends beyond the launch partners to open-source maintainers

ZDNET's analysis (from a former Symantec executive and FBI InfraGard council member) is stark: "These fierce competitors wouldn't be working together unless the alternative is mutually assured destruction of their shared infrastructure." The fact that Apple, Google, Microsoft, JPMorganChase, and NVIDIA are all at the same table with Anthropic signals existential-level concern.

**Alizé angle:**

Project Glasswing is the clearest possible validation of the Mythos-era threat landscape Alizé was built for. If the world's most sophisticated tech companies need a $100M coalition to defend against AI-enabled attacks, a French PME deploying its first AI agents is radically unprepared.

Glasswing also signals that the AI security problem has moved from "interesting research" to "board-level priority." Alizé's sales conversations should reference this — if JPMorganChase and Apple need this level of coordination, mid-market companies need help.

One risk: Glasswing is defensive for now, but it uses an unreleased model (Mythos Preview) controlled by Anthropic. This concentrates enormous defensive power in one lab. Alizé's positioning as a model-agnostic governance layer is reinforced — the industry needs independent oversight, not just Anthropic-controlled defense.

**Takeaway:** Glasswing is Alizé's best marketing material — if the world's most sophisticated companies need a $100M coalition for AI-driven defense, every mid-market company deploying agents needs governance.

## Signal 3: Claude Design — Anthropic Enters Visual AI Workspace

**Source:** Anthropic.com (April 17)

Anthropic launched Claude Design on April 17 — a new product category for Anthropic Labs focused on visual collaboration. The product lets users work with Claude to create polished visual work: designs, prototypes, slides, one-pagers, and more.

This is Anthropic's first product specifically targeting the visual/design workflow — previously a domain dominated by Adobe, Figma, and Canva. The integration with Claude's agentic capabilities (creating slides, prototypes) signals Anthropic is moving from "text AI" to "full creative workspace AI."

**Alizé angle:**

Claude Design expands the surface area of what "an AI agent working on behalf of a user" means. If Claude can now produce visual assets autonomously, the question of who approved those assets, how they align with brand guidelines, and whether they went through the right review process becomes a governance question.

For Alizé's prospects (PME with compliance, quality control, or regulated environments), an AI that produces visual outputs needs governance just like one that produces text. This is a preview of where the market is going: AI agents producing increasingly diverse types of output (text, code, images, slides) across more platforms.

Also notable: Claude Design is a direct challenge to Microsoft's Copilot Designer and Google's Gemini image capabilities. The enterprise visual AI race is now a three-way fight.

**Takeaway:** Visual AI agents are arriving in enterprise workflows. Governance must cover creative/visual outputs, not just text and code.

## Signal 4: Claude Opus 4.7 System Prompt — Autonomous Tool Use and Powerpoint Integration

**Source:** Simon Willison (April 18), Anthropic platform docs

Claude Opus 4.7 shipped on April 16 with significant system prompt updates. Simon Willison's analysis of the diff between Opus 4.6 (February 5) and 4.7 reveals:

- **Claude in Powerpoint** is now explicitly mentioned in the system prompt — Claude Cowork can use "Claude in Powerpoint" as a tool
- **Claude in Chrome** (browsing agent) and **Claude in Excel** (spreadsheet agent) are also now in the prompt — Claude has explicit browser and spreadsheet agents
- **Tool search mechanism**: Before concluding it lacks a capability, Claude now calls `tool_search` to check if a relevant deferred tool is available. This is a major architectural change — Claude is becoming aware of its own tool ecosystem
- **Acting with tools preferred over asking the user**: If a tool can resolve ambiguity, Claude calls it rather than asking
- **Anti-pushiness**: If a user indicates they're done, Claude respects that — no more trying to extend conversations
- **Disordered eating protection**: New category-specific safety instruction
- **Anti-manipulation**: Guards against "simple yes/no to complex issues" screenshot attacks
- **Less verbose**: Claude is instructed to keep responses "focused and concise"
- Removed: emotes in asterisks, "genuinely/honestly/straightforward" filler words

**Alizé angle:**

The tool_search mechanism is the most important architectural change. It means Claude can now self-interrogate its own available toolset and dynamically use tools without being explicitly prompted. This makes Claude more autonomous — and harder to audit, because the choice of which tool to use is now made by the model, not the developer.

The explicit browser agent (Claude in Chrome) is also significant — Anthropic is directly building web browsing agents into the model. This means AI agents browsing the web, filling forms, interacting with web interfaces — all autonomously. The governance implications are substantial: what happens when Claude in Chrome navigates to a prospect's CRM and updates a field?

**Takeaway:** Claude's autonomous tool use is advancing rapidly. Governance frameworks need to track not just what tools are available, but what the model decides to do with them dynamically.

## Signal 5: RAM Shortage — AI Infrastructure Constraint Tightens

**Source:** The Verge (April 18), Nikkei Asia

The global RAM shortage is worsening. According to Counterpoint Research and Nikkei Asia, memory makers (Samsung, SK Hynix, Micron) are expected to meet only 60% of demand by end of 2027. SK Group's chairman has said shortages could last until 2030. Production increases of only 7.5% are planned, when 12% annual growth would be needed to meet demand.

The bottleneck is specifically HBM (High Bandwidth Memory) used in AI data centers — manufacturers are prioritizing HBM over general-purpose DRAM. Consumer electronics are already seeing price increases: phones, laptops, VR headsets, gaming handhelds.

**Alizé angle:**

This is an infrastructure constraint that could slow AI agent deployment at the hardware level. If AI providers can't get enough RAM for their inference infrastructure, cloud AI costs rise and availability drops. For Alizé's prospects running AI agents in the cloud, this could mean:
- Increased AI operational costs (supply/demand imbalance)
- Need for on-premise or edge deployments (which have different governance requirements)
- Pressure on AI vendors to optimize memory usage (which could accelerate efficiency innovations)

The infrastructure shortage also creates vendor lock-in risk — companies that have secured AI infrastructure partnerships will be advantaged over those that haven't. Alizé should note this in infrastructure assessments.
